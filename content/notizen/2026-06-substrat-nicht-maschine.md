## The decision

In the texts of this project, the word *Sprachsubstrat* — language substrate — stands where others say *AI*, *LLM*, or *assistant*. This is **a decision, not a quirk**. It calls for explanation, because the chosen word is unfamiliar and the customary ones are so familiar that their aftertaste scarcely registers.

This note brings the decision into the light.

## What a large language model does

A large language model is trained on very many texts and returns, in response to a prompt, a text that is statistically close to the patterns it saw in training. It operates on the surface of language — on what Saussure called the *signifiant*: the sign, not the signified. It knows word distributions, sentence shapes, idiomatic turns. **It does not know what the sentences mean.** In Benveniste's terms: it produces *énoncés* without the act of *énonciation* — utterances without the speech-act in which a speaker refers to a world.

That is the technical description. It is sober, not dismissive. Everything that follows rests on it.

## What *Machine* misses

The word *machine* works as an image — the printing press, the typewriter, the thing in front of one. It works less well when the phenomenon lies *within* language. A printing press is mechanics. The language model is statistics over writing that was already language. It is **closer to a material than to a tool**. Whoever addresses it as *machine* easily hangs a function on it that it does not have.

## What *AI* misses

*AI* — *artificial intelligence* — projects human traits onto something non-human. The model has no consciousness, no experience, no standpoint, no world to which it could anchor itself. It imitates the linguistic surface of cognitive acts at great speed. That is striking, and it is not intelligence in the sense the word carried before 2020. Whoever keeps using the word smuggles in a comparability that the matter does not support. The downstream terms — *AI collaboration*, *AI assistance*, *AI-assisted writing*, *human-AI writing* — extend the comparability and posit **two subjects where there is one**.

## What *LLM* misses

*LLM* — *large language model* — is the correct technical designation, and the term that has taken the place of *AI* inside the technical speech community. One might expect a model to carry less personification than an intelligence. In the language as it is actually spoken, it does not. *The LLM says that…* — *the LLM answered* — *the LLM doesn't know that*. The subject-seam has come along. *LLM* has displaced *AI* in the developer forums without shedding the anthropomorphizing reflex. What ought to mean a statistical model becomes **a speaker in a conversation**. Whoever listens knows: what is meant is rarely *a matrix of probabilities* but rather *the thing one talks to*.

## What *Substrate* captures

*AI* and *LLM*, each in its own way, draw the same line: from what processes language, a speaker is made. The word *substrate* draws another.

It is the Latin word for *underlayer*, *that which lies beneath*. In chemistry, it is the matter an enzyme acts upon. In biology, the ground on which something grows. In linguistics, what lies beneath a language and acts through it. In every one of these uses, the word has the same sense: <u>material that is supplied, without itself being the operation</u>.

*Sprachsubstrat* carries this sense over to the language model. It supplies material — the statistically likely formulation. **What happens with the material is decided by the person at the desk.** The act is *mediation*, the result is *machine-mediated writing*.

Three things the word secures that *AI* and *LLM* do not.

**First.** It avoids the subject-attribution at the root, not only on the surface of polite phrasing. A substrate *supplies*, *carries*, *stands by*. It *wills* nothing, *says* nothing. Responsibility for what stands at the end rests with the one who writes.

**Second.** It makes a lineage visible. *Writing technology* — printing press, typewriter, now substrate — is a line in which the substrate is a new but not a first member. Every one of these stations changed *how* texts come into being without doing away with the one who writes. The substrate is **not the exception but the next chapter**.

**Third.** It is abstract enough to mean more than today's language model. What arrives tomorrow — other architectures, other shapes, other vendors — will fall under the same term, as long as it occupies the same position in the arrangement of writing.

## What would be lost

The word is unfamiliar. It is a threshold. Whoever adopts the standard learns it; whoever reads a note should not fail at it. For that reason, *Sprachsubstrat* is briefly glossed at its first appearance in every note — *material that a language model supplies* — and then used. Whoever wants to know it more precisely finds the derivation here.

The other words — *AI*, *LLM*, *language model* — are not forbidden. They appear in occasion-blocks where a cited text uses them. They appear in this note. But they do not carry the institutional position. That position is carried by the substrate term, because only that term carries the speech-act of self-declaration cleanly: *the author says how much of the language substrate has gone into the work*. With *AI* or *LLM*, it would be a collaboration ledger, in each case with a different shade. With *Substrate*, it is what it is: **an account of material**.
