## Writing Technology

*Maschinenschrift* (German: machine-script) is writing technology in its next stage. The printing press was followed by the typewriter, the typewriter by the language substrate. Each of these stations changed *how* texts come into being, without doing away with the one who writes. Whoever operates a typewriter writes — no one would claim the machine is the author. Whoever writes on a language substrate writes likewise. The machine is medium, not subject.

The word *Maschinenschrift* belongs to this lineage. In the twentieth century, it named the typescript that came out of a typewriter — text that had passed through a technical apparatus, written by a human, mediated by a machine. The same form now names the next chapter.

## Language Substrate

We call what the machine delivers the **language substrate** *(Sprachsubstrat)* — a word that grants the machine no intelligence it does not have. It names material: language made available through a technical procedure. The substrate is apparatus, not counterpart — closer to a printing press than to an editor. What happens to the material is decided by the person at the desk.

The substrate has no intention and no standpoint. It delivers the statistically likely formulation — the condensation of all texts on which it was trained into a surface from which what lies near a prompt can be retrieved. It is echo, not voice.

In social terms, the substrate carries the form of an **aggregated anonymity** *(aggregierte Anonymität)* — no identifiable other voice, but the statistical sum of all voices in the training corpus. The reader can no longer ask *who* wrote this and expect a single answer — as long as they do not know that someone wrote through the substrate.

Whoever writes through the substrate writes **mediated** — as all writing is mediated by writing technologies, only more densely and at a level unknown to earlier mediations. The act is *mediation*, the result is *machine-mediated writing*.

## The Seal

The seal is the notation in which a work declares its mediation. It consists of two square brackets and three rows — five slots per row, dots or empty.

Three axes carry the statement:

- **Diction** (D) — how much the substrate contributed to the linguistic surface. Idiom, sentence flow, stylistic register. Scale 0–5.
- ***Geist*** (G) — German for *spirit* in the sense of Hegel and *Geisteswissenschaften*; how much the substrate contributed to the work's *Geist*. Ideas, arguments, structural decisions, content. Scale 0–5.
- **Correspondence** (K) — whether the work is documented in the bibliographic world. Binary: *belegt* (documented) or *ausstehend* (pending).

An **empty seal** `[ ]` is the default position: the author was alone, no substrate involved. Dots count what was imported — not what was achieved. The axes measure the machine's contribution, not the human's. This polarity is the institution's foundational decision. The writing human is the unmarked default.

Correspondence stands in a logic of its own. Where Diction and *Geist* measure mediation, Correspondence checks a relation: whether the work the declaration refers to stands in the bibliographic world — findable, datable, identifiable. As long as this is not confirmed, the seal carries a **half-stroke** on the right of the lower row — marker for *ausstehend*. The axis is no verdict; it is the narrow point at which Maschinenschrift binds itself, in a controlled way, back to the world.

## Geist aus der Maschine

The project's diagnostic formula is *Geist aus der Maschine* — an inversion of the ancient *deus ex machina*. There, a foreign resolution was lowered into the play; here, language arrives with a **Geist-effect** whose origin is not the person at the desk, but a statistical echo of the entire history of writing up to the substrate's training cut-off. The G-axis answers the formula: the dots show how much *Geist* was imported from the substrate.

An empty seal says: none. The *Geist* comes entirely from the author. Dots count what was added.

## What Maschinenschrift Addresses — and What It Does Not

Maschinenschrift records three things:

- **Authenticity** of self-disclosure — that the work has declared its own origin.
- **Mediation** — made visible through the two dot rows of the seal.
- **Voice** — indirectly, because the visibility of the mediated lets the unmediated become recognizable.

Maschinenschrift expressly does *not* record the following:

- the question on what language models may be trained,
- the concentration of linguistic infrastructure in few corporations,
- the ecological footprint of model operation,
- the loss of cognitive capacities through habitual delegation,
- the truth of individual factual claims in the text.

This self-restriction is the condition under which the system remains broadly compatible. Whoever rejects language models, whoever works with them, whoever considers both possible — all three benefit from a form in which mediation is documentable, without the form forcing an ethical stance.

## Registry

Whoever adopts the standard sets the seal. Whoever wants to appear in the **registry** sends an email. The registry is no premium label, no editorial filter; it gathers works whose self-declaration is plausible and whose bibliographic existence is verifiable.

The form is not new. The *Stationers' Register* recorded, from the sixteenth century onward, which printer printed which work. Maschinenschrift extends this tradition to the next stage of mediation: not *who printed* but *who mediated and to what degree*.

A list of registered works stands under [Registry](/registry/). The first work is *[La Scuola dei Silenzi](/registry/la-scuola-dei-silenzi/)* by I. O. Vargas, declared D=4, G=2, Correspondence pending.
